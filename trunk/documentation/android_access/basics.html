Navigation

Android is designed to be mostly navigable with keys, without needing to use the touch screen. So for the most part you won't be using special commands that aren't available to other users: you'll be using mostly the same keystrokes. The only difference is that you'll get speech feedback instead of relying on seeing the screen to know what you're doing.

Almost every Android phone has some type of directional controller: most commonly a clickable trackball, arrow keys, or a directional pad. You'll use that controller to move around and click on things. Depending on what accessibility applications you've enabled, you'll get speech, sound, and haptic (vibrational) feedback to tell you what you've selected and what's happening on your phone.

There are three other keys that you will use frequently. Make sure you know where these keys are on your phone:

  • The Home button will take you to the home screen, where you can launch applications, open the notification panel, and much more. While Android comes with a home screen that's reasonably accessible, you can download other alternative apps to use as your home screen. In addition, if you press and hold the Home key, it will bring up a window that lets you switch to a recently opened app.
  • The Back button will take you back to the previous screen, whether in the same app or a different app. As an example, if you're reading an email, pressing Back might take you to your Inbox, pressing Back again might take you to your list of mailboxes, and pressing Back again yet might take you to your home screen. You can use Back to close most dialogs or exit most screens that are causing you problems.
  • The Menu button opens a menu specific to the current screen. Quite a bit of Android functionality is only possible if you open a menu, so be sure to check for it. Menus are usually two-dimensional, so use the arrows to move up, down, left and right to find all of the options in the menu, and click one if you want. To close a menu, press Back.

The home screen

There are a few very important items in the menu on the home screen: Settings and Notifications. You may have already found the Settings, that's where you enable accessibility. You'll find all sorts of other useful customization options in Settings, and as soon as you have time you should explore them and customize your phone. Notifications are one of the primary ways that Android communicates messages to you without interrupting you too much. Example notifications include new mail, a new text message, or a low battery warning, but any app can post a notification. When you have TalkBack or another accessibility service running, it will speak the notification when it first appears, but then whenever you want you can open the notifications from the home screen menu to review all of your notifications. Most notifications are clickable - when you click them they'll take you directly to the message in question or tell you more information about the warning.

The other most important thing on the home screen is the application launcher. On older versions of Android it might be called "Sliding Drawer", and on newer versions it's just called "All Applications". When you click it, it opens a window of all of your applications.

One thing that will be tricky at first is that this view is two-dimensional, with four apps per row. In order to find all of your apps, you'll need to explore all four apps in each horizontal row. If this bothers you, you can install alternate home screens or app launchers, such as the Eyes-Free shell.

Unlocking gesture

There are two scenarios where you may need to use the touch screen: unlocking the phone after waking it up and answering a phone call. In both cases, you just need to learn one simple gesture: while holding the phone in its tall orientation, press your finger near the bottom of the left side of the screen and swipe it all the way to the right. To test this, first lock your phone by pressing the power button once - TalkBack says Screen Off. Then press the power button again and your screen will come on, but the phone will still be locked. If you have KickBack running, you'll get feedback that helps you find the handle you need to drag. Just keep touching the screen until you feel the phone do a single quick vibrational bump. When you feel it, hold your finger down and slide it to the right until you get more feedback and the screen unlocks.

Making phone calls

This same gesture used to unlock is used to answer a call. To decline a call, do the same gesture in reverse, from right to left. Of course, some phones like the HTC G1 and LG Ally have Call Start and Call End buttons, in which case you don't need these gestures.

If you don't like swiping to unlock your phone, you can disable this or choose a different unlocking strategy. See Customizing for more details.

Ending a call is accessible, but if your phone doesn't have a physical End Call button, doing so quickly without exploring the screen is more of a challenge.

  • If you have Android 2.2 or later, there's a checkbox in the Accessibility settings that makes the power button hang up calls.
  • If you have Android 2.1 or earlier, you can try to learn where to press the touch-screen: it's in the center, towards the bottom. Of course, you can always use your directional pad to explore all of the buttons on the screen and click the one to end the call.

Everything else about phone calls is already accessible if your phone has a keyboard: dialing, editing contacts, etc.

Now you know everything you need to start using your phone! The rest of this guide will go into much more detail about some common things you might want to do with your phone, and how to customize your experience to work just right for you.

At this point, many of your questions may not be specific to accessibility. If you want to learn how to make phone calls, synchronize your contacts, play music, or more, you don't need a special guide for accessibility. You can just read the manual that came with your phone, or search the Internet for general advice on using Android. In many cases, your experience may not be very different than the experience for any other user.
