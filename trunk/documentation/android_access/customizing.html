Customizing your experience

Up: Android Accessibility
Next: Accessibility Services

One of the best things about Android is that almost everything is customizable. This is something that all users can enjoy, but it's particularly helpful for accessibility, because just about anything on the phone that's inaccessible or not fully accessible can be replaced by another app that's more accessible.

Here are some of the types of apps that you might want to install, and some examples of particular apps to search for:

  • Voices: the voice that comes with Android is based on the SVOX Pico engine, but you're not limited to using just that one. There are currently two other choices on the market. Espeak is another free voice. It sounds a little unusual, but it's free, fast, and supports dozens of languages. In addition, there's a commercial voice from Loquendo, called Susan. Just search for either Susan or Loquendo on the Market. While it's not free, it's not very expensive and the quality is very good. Installing a voice requires a couple of extra steps that a normal app doesn't require: first open the app to download any voice data and finish the installation, then open Settings and enable the voice you want to use.

    If you have Android 2.1 or earlier, you can install TTS Extended, which allows apps to have all of the TTS capabilities of Android 2.2. It won't do anything by itself, but some talking apps might require it or give you more speech options if you have it installed. You don't need it if you're running Android 2.2 or higher.

  • Accessibility Tools: Your Android device probably came with a version of TalkBack pre-installed. However, TalkBack is under active development, and if you want the latest features, you should download TalkBack from the Market. Once you download the Market version of TalkBack, the Market will automatically notify you when new versions are available, from then on. Note that TalkBack cannot be uninstalled.

    In addition, there's another screen reader available, called Spiel. Currently Spiel is not available on the Market, but you can download it directly by searching for the URL http://tinyurl.com/aspiel from the home screen. While the url will load in the Browser, which is not accessible, it will automatically download the app and you can install it by going to your Notifications screen. You can have both TalkBack and Spiel installed at the same time, and then enable whichever one you want to use. It's perfectly safe to have both of them enabled, but only one of them will actually work at a time. Spiel and TalkBack work very much the same way, so you shouldn't expect major differences. They're both being actively developed independently, and they both have their strengths and weaknesses.

    More information on the Accessibility Services page.

  • Screen lock: If you don't like swiping across the screen, you can install applications that eliminate the need to do a gesture in order to unlock the screen, or replace it with a separate lock screen requiring a password. Two Market apps to get you started: "No Lock" is a simple app with a single checkbox - checked to require unlocking the screen with a swipe, unchecked to not require it. "Rock Lock" is a screen that lets you listen to music while the phone is locked. It also makes a great music player! Search the market for other lock screens!
  • Home screen: The Eyes-free shell is a home screen designed to be used without looking at the screen. It uses an innovative touch-screen input method that lets you access a lot of functionality without being able to see the screen, but without needing to first open your keyboard. Press and hold your finger near the center of the screen, then pretend you're in the center of a 3 x 3 grid of squares. Move your finger to each of the 9 cells in the square and listen to what each one is, then release your finger when you find something you want. It installs a separate app called Shortcuts Manager for Eyes-Free Shell that lets you customize it.

    For more information on how to use the Eyes-free shell and other Eyes-free Android apps, see the Eyes-free YouTube videos.

  • Email: currently there's one accessible email program, called IDEAL Accessible K9 mail.
  • Web: For more information on how to browse the web with Android, see this news release from the IDEAL Group and the Web Access Plug-In.
